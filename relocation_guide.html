Part One

SWOT Analysis — Current Situation

An honest assessment of what living alone in Headingley offers, and where its limitations lie.

Living alone in her own home, Headingley Leeds

Strengths

What works well

  • Deeply familiar surroundings and long-established routines
  • Full independence and autonomy over daily decisions
  • Personal belongings provide comfort and identity
  • Established community — neighbours, GP, local shops, faith life
  • Headingley is a vibrant, walkable area with strong community
  • Property equity retained in full
  • No ongoing care home fees
Weaknesses

Where gaps exist now

  • High social isolation risk when living alone at 85
  • No immediate help available in emergencies
  • Home may have stairs, narrow doors, bathroom hazards
  • Medication management relies entirely on self
  • Ongoing property maintenance costs and responsibility
  • Utility costs for a large property on a fixed income
Opportunities

What could improve things here

  • Home adaptations via Leeds City Council OT assessment
  • Personal alarm / telecare technology already available
  • Domiciliary care package could be arranged
  • Attendance Allowance (if not already claimed) adds income
  • Neighbours and community networks can be formalised
Threats

Risks that grow over time

  • Falls risk increases with age — no one present to respond
  • Cognitive or physical decline may make current home unsafe rapidly
  • Isolation deepens as mobility declines
  • Emergency admission to hospital may force a rushed move decision
  • Financial exploitation risk if vulnerable and living alone

Part Two

SWOT Analysis — Supported Living

A clear-eyed look at what supported living in Yorkshire can offer — and what it genuinely costs in more than financial terms.

Residential, extra care, or assisted living in Yorkshire

Strengths

What gets better

  • 24-hour staffing and emergency response
  • Medication management handled professionally
  • Built-in social community — reduces isolation significantly
  • No home maintenance responsibility whatsoever
  • Activities programme, communal dining, peer friendships
  • Care scales up as needs increase — no need to move again
Weaknesses

What is genuinely lost

  • Full independence and autonomy over daily routines
  • Familiar community ties in Headingley are severed
  • Much smaller living space than a house
  • Pets may not be permitted
  • Care can feel impersonal; staff turnover disrupts relationships
  • Limited ability to personalise the space
Opportunities

What Yorkshire offers

  • Excellent settings in Harrogate, York, Ilkley, Skipton, Ripon
  • Local authority may contribute if assets below threshold
  • Attendance Allowance contributes regardless of means
  • Deferred Payment Agreement allows property to be sold later
  • New peer community could be richer than current isolation
Threats

Risks to manage carefully

  • Potentially high fees (£1,200–£2,000+/week residential)
  • Care quality varies enormously between settings
  • Property sale may be required to fund care long-term
  • Adjustment period can be significant — first 3 months are hardest
  • A forced or rushed move worsens outcomes markedly

Part Three

Types of Supported Living

Before researching specific locations, it is worth identifying the right level of support. These are not interchangeable options — each serves a distinct profile of need.

Lowest support

Sheltered Housing

Independent flat with a warden or scheme manager nearby. Very low care input. Best if still very capable but wants the reassurance of support on hand. Often leasehold purchase.

Moderate support

Extra Care / Assisted Living

Self-contained flat within a complex with on-site care available but not 24-hour institutional. Retains high autonomy. Best when independent but needing some daily support. Typically lease or purchase.

Full support

Residential Care Home

24-hour personal care with meals, cleaning and laundry included. No nursing unless dual-registered. Most appropriate when personal care needs are moderate to high. Weekly fee basis.

Nursing care

Nursing Home

As residential, but with registered nurses on-site. Appropriate for complex medical needs, dementia care, or post-hospital recovery. Highest cost tier. NHS may fund some or all in some circumstances.

Part Four

The Twelve Stages

Every stage from first conversation to settled new chapter. Click any stage to read the detail. Stage 9 — preparing the current home — is expanded fully below.

Stage 01

Initial Conversations & Consent

Weeks 1–3 · The foundation of everything
›
The most important stage. Nothing can proceed meaningfully without your aunt's genuine engagement. At 85, she retains full legal capacity unless assessed otherwise — all decisions must be made with her, not for her.

Actions

  • Have an honest, unhurried conversation about her current challenges. Listen more than you advise.
  • Acknowledge what she values most about her home and life in Headingley — don't minimise these things.
  • Introduce supported living as an option, not a conclusion already reached by the family.
  • Agree on what you are collectively trying to achieve: safety, companionship, reducing burden, proximity to family.
  • Agree that she will be involved in every single decision that follows.
  • Identify whether a Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) is in place — and if not, treat this as urgent.
If there is no LPA in place: Contact a solicitor immediately to arrange both a health/welfare LPA and a property/finance LPA. This is one of the most important legal protections available and must be completed while capacity is unambiguous.
Stage 02

Care Needs Assessment

Weeks 2–4 · Establishing the right level of support
›

Before researching locations, establish what level of care she actually needs. This prevents placing her in a setting that is over- or under-resourced — a setting too high in care can feel infantilising; one too low can leave her unsafe within months.

Actions

  • Request a free Care Needs Assessment from Leeds City Council Adult Social Care (0113 222 4401) — this is a legal right under the Care Act 2014.
  • Ask her GP for a written summary of current medical conditions, medications, and any anticipated changes.
  • Request an Occupational Therapist (OT) assessment of her current home — this will identify specific risk factors and inform what any new setting would need.
  • If there are any cognitive changes, request a memory assessment via the GP before proceeding further.
  • Document across key domains: mobility, continence, personal hygiene, medication management, nutrition, communication, and social engagement.

The outcome of this stage will clarify whether sheltered housing, extra care, residential, or nursing is the right destination — and whether the state will contribute to costs.

Stage 03

Financial Assessment & Planning

Weeks 3–6 · Understanding the full picture
›

Assets and income inventory

  • Current property value — arrange free valuations from 2–3 estate agents
  • Savings, ISAs, investments, premium bonds
  • All pension income: State Pension, private, occupational
  • Current benefits: is she claiming Attendance Allowance? (Up to £108.55/week — not means-tested. Apply now if not.)
  • Pension Credit, Council Tax Reduction, any disability benefits

Means testing

In England (2024/25), if capital assets exceed £23,250 (including property value once in a care home), she will be self-funding. Below this threshold, Leeds City Council may contribute to assessed care needs. The Deferred Payment Agreement (DPA) scheme allows the property to act as security so she need not sell immediately.

Key professionals to engage

  • A SOLLA-accredited financial adviser (Society of Later Life Advisers: societyoflaterlifeadvisers.co.uk) — specialist in later-life planning
  • Use the Turn2us benefits calculator (turn2us.org.uk) for a full benefits check
  • Age UK Leeds offers a free welfare benefits check: 0113 389 3000
Stage 04

Research & Longlist

Weeks 4–8 · Exploring Yorkshire's options
›

Yorkshire is a large county with excellent care provision. She doesn't want to leave the county — and there is no need to. The following areas are all within comfortable reach of Leeds and offer strong options:

Areas worth exploring

  • Within Leeds: North Leeds (Roundhay, Moortown, Alwoodley), Horsforth, Guiseley, Wetherby
  • Harrogate — consistently highly regarded for quality of life; strong care sector; 20 minutes from Leeds
  • York — historic, vibrant, excellent care options; 25 minutes by train
  • Ilkley / Skipton — smaller, scenic, strong community feel, good care provision
  • Ripon, Knaresborough, Boroughbridge — quieter market towns with established care homes

Research tools

  • CQC website (cqc.org.uk) — filter by area, type, and rating. Aim for Good or Outstanding.
  • carehome.co.uk — resident and family reviews alongside CQC data
  • Age UK Yorkshire & Humberside (ageukyorkshire.org.uk) — impartial local guidance
  • Leeds City Council and relevant local authority approved provider lists

Build a longlist of 8–12 options, noting: type of setting, CQC rating and inspection date, fee range, specialist provision, distance, and current availability.

Stage 05

Shortlisting & Visits

Weeks 6–12 · Seeing it in person
›

Narrow the longlist to 4–6 settings for in-person visits. She should attend every visit if at all possible — this is her home, not a decision being made on her behalf.

Questions to ask each setting

  • What is the staff-to-resident ratio during the day and overnight?
  • What is the staff turnover rate? (High turnover is a warning sign.)
  • How is the care plan reviewed, and how often?
  • What happens if her needs increase — would she need to move again?
  • Can she bring her own furniture? Are pets permitted?
  • What does a typical day look like? What activities are available?
  • What is the fee increase policy? What extras are charged additionally?

What to observe on the visit

  • Is the atmosphere warm and calm, or busy and clinical?
  • Do staff know residents by name?
  • Are residents engaged, or sitting passively in corridors?
  • Is there natural light, outdoor space, pleasant communal areas?
  • Are there residents who seem similar in character to your aunt?

Score each setting across: care quality, atmosphere, location, facilities, value, and gut feeling. Get her honest reaction after each visit.

Stage 06

Trial / Respite Stay

Weeks 10–14 · Optional but strongly recommended
›

Many residential settings offer short respite stays, typically 1–2 weeks. This is one of the most valuable steps in the entire process — it allows her to genuinely experience daily life in the setting before committing to it permanently.

Actions

  • Arrange a respite stay at the top 1–2 shortlisted settings.
  • Debrief honestly and unhurriedly afterwards: what did she enjoy? What concerned her? Would she feel at home there long-term?
  • Ask the home for their honest assessment: how did she settle? Is her profile a good match for the setting?
  • If one setting clearly outperforms, this simplifies the decision in Stage 7 considerably.
Even a few days in the setting is worth many hours of desk research. A place that reads well on paper sometimes feels wrong in person, and vice versa.
Stage 07

The Decision

Weeks 12–16 · Only when she is ready
›
Only proceed when she is satisfied — or at minimum, genuinely accepting. Forced moves are associated with significantly worse outcomes in elderly people. Research consistently shows that a sense of agency in the decision improves settling-in and long-term wellbeing.

Formalising the choice

  • Request the full resident contract from the chosen home. Have a solicitor review it before signing.
  • Confirm: notice period, fee review clause, what is included vs. charged as extras, what happens if needs escalate.
  • Confirm a start date and whether the room can be held on a deposit.
  • If the council is contributing to costs, they must formally agree the placement and the fee level — this can take several weeks.
Stage 08

Legal & Administrative Preparation

Weeks 12–20 · Runs in parallel with Stages 6–7
›

Legal checklist

  • Lasting Power of Attorney (health/welfare AND property/finance) — register with Office of the Public Guardian if not done
  • Will — review and update with a solicitor if circumstances have changed
  • Review any existing trust arrangements

Administrative checklist

  • Notify HMRC, DWP (pension, benefits) of change of address
  • Register with a new GP surgery near the chosen setting
  • Request transfer of NHS records to new GP
  • Notify all bank and financial institutions
  • Arrange Royal Mail redirection from Headingley address (runs up to 12 months)
  • Cancel or transfer utilities: electricity, gas, broadband, TV licence
  • Cancel local subscriptions: milk, newspapers, parish magazine
  • Inform home insurer that the property is changing occupancy status
  • Contact new local authority to transfer any council-funded care package

Stage 10

The Move

Target weeks 20–28 · The day itself
›

Two weeks before

  • Confirm all logistics with the new home: move-in time, parking, key handover procedure
  • Pack personal items carefully — label everything clearly
  • Arrange for a familiar, calm person to be present on the day itself
  • Prepare a 'day bag': medications, phone charger, familiar photographs, favourite mug, toiletries

On move day

  • Keep the pace calm and unhurried — this is not a logistics operation, it is a life moment
  • Arrive with her at the new home; do not send belongings ahead without her
  • Help set up her room so it feels personal immediately: photographs on the wall, her own bedding, her favourite chair in position
  • Meet the named key worker who will be her primary contact
  • Stay for a meal with her if at all possible
  • Before leaving, agree and confirm the next visit date — do not leave without her knowing when she will see you again
Stage 11

The Settling-In Period

Months 1–3 · The most vulnerable window
›
Research consistently shows that frequent family contact and social engagement in the first three months significantly improves long-term outcomes for older adults moving to care settings.

Actions

  • Visit as frequently as practical — ideally weekly for the first month
  • Phone or video call on days you cannot visit
  • Encourage engagement in at least one activity she genuinely enjoys
  • Ask the home for regular feedback on how she is settling
  • Monitor: appetite, mood, engagement, medication management, sleep
  • If she is unhappy, take it seriously — distinguish between normal adjustment (very common) and genuine distress or unmet needs (requires action)
  • Raise any concerns formally with the home manager in writing, keeping a copy
Stage 12

Ongoing Review & Advocacy

Ongoing · Quarterly and annual rhythms
›

Every three months

  • Request a care plan review meeting with the key worker
  • Review whether her needs have changed and whether the setting remains appropriate
  • Check finances: confirm correct benefits, fees as contracted, no unexpected charges

Annually

  • Full financial review with SOLLA-accredited adviser
  • Review LPA and Will if circumstances have changed
  • Check the home's current CQC rating on cqc.org.uk — ratings can change

Advocacy and escalation

  • If unhappy with care, use the home's internal complaints procedure first
  • If unresolved, escalate to the Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman (lgo.org.uk)
  • For safeguarding concerns, contact the Care Quality Commission directly
  • The local authority can arrange an independent advocate if needed

Stage Nine — Full Detail

Preparing the Current Home

Often the most emotionally demanding part of the entire process. Allow 8–12 weeks. Treat it as a journey of many sessions, never a single intensive task.

"We're not clearing your home — we're choosing what comes with you into your next chapter."

Step 9.1

Emotional preparation — before touching anything

Before a single drawer is opened, have a direct conversation about what this process means to her. Acknowledge openly that letting go of a home is a significant loss, even when the move is the right decision. Some people find it liberating; many find it grief-like.

Give her ownership of the process. She decides what goes — not family members making quiet judgements about what is "worth keeping." One room per session, over several weeks, with breaks. Never all at once.

Step 9.2

Measure the new space first

Before sorting a single item, get precise measurements of her new room or flat. Most residential care rooms are 12–16 square metres. Extra care flats are typically 35–55 sq metres. You need to know:

  • Exact room dimensions and door widths
  • Whether she can bring her own bed (many homes prefer this; some supply them)
  • Wall space for pictures and shelving
  • Storage available — wardrobe, drawers, cupboards
  • Whether there is any outdoor space or balcony

Armed with measurements, you can make honest decisions about what will actually fit — and avoid the heartbreak of moving a beloved armchair only to find it blocks the doorway.

Step 9.3

Sorting belongings — the four-category framework

Work room by room. Use four physical areas — boxes, bags, or floor sections — labelled clearly:

  • Goes with her: Favourite chair, bedside lamp, photographs (curated), personal jewellery, her own bedding, radio or TV, a few beloved ornaments, books, daily-use items
  • Gifted to family: Go around the family with her involvement. Let her give things with intention while she can see them appreciated — far better than distribution after the fact
  • Donated: Age UK Leeds (free collection for larger items), British Heart Foundation furniture and electricals, Leeds Furniture Store (distributes to people in need locally — particularly meaningful)
  • Sold: Items of value — consider Morphets of Harrogate or Tennants Auctioneers (Leyburn) for antiques, furniture, jewellery, art. Facebook Marketplace for everyday items.
  • Disposed: Whatever remains — Leeds City Council bulky waste collection (0113 222 4408), or a house clearance company for the full residual at the end
Step 9.4

Documents and paperwork

Every home accumulates decades of paperwork. Go through it carefully — important documents are often mixed with old utility bills.

Locate and secure:

  • Will (original — store with solicitor)
  • LPA documents (both originals)
  • Property deeds or mortgage discharge document
  • Birth and marriage certificates, passport
  • National Insurance documentation, pension letters
  • Insurance policies (home, life, others)
  • Premium bonds (notify NS&I of change of address)
  • Share certificates or investment documents

Shred (do not bin):

  • Old bank statements, utility bills
  • Anything with full name, address, account numbers, or National Insurance number
Identity fraud note: Targeting recently relocated or deceased people via unsecured paper records is not uncommon. A cross-cut shredder is essential, or use a professional shredding service.
Step 9.5

Managing the property itself

If selling: Instruct an estate agent 8–12 weeks before you want to complete. Properties in Headingley (LS6) generally sell well given strong demand. Active local agents include Linley & Simpson, Manning Stainton, Dacre Son & Hartley, and Ryder & Dutton. Get 2–3 valuations. A professional deep clean (£150–£300) usually pays for itself. The property does not need to sell before she moves — she can move first, which removes pressure from both processes.

If letting: Appoint a full management letting agent (10–15% of monthly rent) so no burden falls on family. Ensure compliance: gas safety certificate, EICR, EPC, smoke and CO alarms. Inform the insurer — standard home insurance is invalid for let properties.

If standing empty temporarily: Inform the insurer (most policies void after 30–60 days unoccupied without endorsement). Arrange weekly inspections. Keep heating at minimum 12°C in winter. Redirect mail immediately.

Step 9.6

Utility and service disconnection checklist

  • Gas and electricity — take final meter readings on the day she leaves; close or transfer accounts
  • Water — notify Yorkshire Water (0345 124 2424)
  • Broadband and landline — 30 days' notice usually required; check early termination charges
  • TV Licence — contact TV Licensing to cancel; a refund may be due
  • Council Tax — notify Leeds City Council; 100% exemption for first 6 months on an empty property
  • Royal Mail redirection — set up from Headingley address (around £40 for 3 months, up to 12 months available)
  • Cancel local subscriptions — milk, newspapers, parish magazine, any club memberships
Step 9.7

Professional help

Removal company: Look for BAR-accredited firms (bar.co.uk) with specific experience in elderly relocations. They understand small room sizes, fragile items, and the need for an unhurried pace. Expect £400–£1,000 depending on volume and distance. Get three written quotes.

Decluttering specialists: APDO-accredited professional organisers (apdo.co.uk) work sensitively with older clients. Some later-life moving specialists offer an end-to-end service covering sorting, donating, selling, cleaning, and handover.

House clearance: For whatever remains after all sorting is complete. Ask whether they donate, recycle, or landfill. Check reviews carefully. Expect £200–£600 for a full house.

Step 9.8

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Doing it all at once. One intensive family weekend is almost always counterproductive — distressing, prone to regretted decisions, and exhausting for everyone. Spread it over weeks.
  • Family disagreements over items. Establish from the start that her wishes are final on every item. Family disagreements should happen away from her, before the process begins.
  • Discarding documents without checking. Old-looking paper can contain live share certificates, premium bonds, or insurance policies with real value.
  • Leaving the clearance until after the move. A task hanging over the family for months adds strain. Aim to have the home largely prepared before her move date.
  • Underestimating the emotional cost. Even when she is broadly positive about moving, the day she hands over the keys for the last time can be devastating. Be present for it. Mark it. A final cup of tea, a walk around the garden, a moment to acknowledge what this home has given her.
